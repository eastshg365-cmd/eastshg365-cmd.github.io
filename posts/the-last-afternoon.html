Part A · Informal Proposal Week 10 — May 2026

Project 2 Proposal:
"The Last Afternoon"

A mixed reality experience that reconstructs a moment suspended between existence and memory.

Photo: Peter Herrmann / Unsplash

There is a specific kind of grief that has no name.

It's the feeling of standing in a room you are about to leave forever — knowing that every familiar object around you will soon belong to someone else, or to no one. You can see everything clearly. But it already feels like a memory.

"The room won't exist anymore.
But for 2–3 minutes in VR,
you'll be the last person to stand in it."

"The Last Afternoon" is a mixed reality experience set inside a childhood bedroom on the final afternoon before a family moves out. The room is intact — furniture, scattered objects, afternoon light through the window. But it is also somehow already gone.

The user stands at the centre of this space for approximately 2–3 minutes. There is no narrative. No beginning or end. Only the moment itself — ambient sound, soft light, the texture of familiar things — looping gently, like a photograph you can walk inside.

· · ·

Why this idea

The project assignment asks us to think about "a moment in time" — not as a literal clock event, but as something felt: a transition, a suspension, a routine that means something bigger. This piece is about the threshold between having and losing. The longest short moment.

Recent research on VR and memory is part of what made this concept feel worth pursuing. A 2025 study by Danry et al. found that walking through personal spaces reconstructed in VR — spaces built from video recordings of someone's past — significantly increased the sense of "travelling back," the vividness of recalled memories, and their emotional intensity, compared to simply watching the same footage on a flat screen.[1]

That idea stays with me: that a space you can stand inside does something to memory that a screen cannot. This project tries to make that into an experience — not a therapeutic tool, but an artistic one.

· · ·

What I'm connecting this to

Psychogeography. Guy Debord's psychogeography asks how specific environments shape our emotions and behaviour. This project reconstructs a space that no longer exists, asking: can you feel a place through VR presence the same way you once felt it in real life? The dérive involves drifting through unknown urban space. This project inverts that — the user is fixed, immobile, inside deeply familiar space that has become strange.

Do-Ho Suh. The Korean artist creates fabric replicas of homes, corridors, stairwells — spaces that can be folded and carried. His work is about how a space continues to live in the body long after it is physically gone. I'm doing something analogous using 3D scanning: not to document a space, but to carry it forward as an emotional artifact.

Presence and embodiment. The central design question is what makes the user feel genuinely present in this space — not just inside a picture, but inside a place. Spatial audio plays a key role here. Research by Mavridou et al. (2025) found that spatial audio enhancements in VR measurably increased both self-reported presence and physiological arousal — particularly when the audio was rich and task-relevant.[2] For this piece, that means the faint creak of the building, the hum of an old fan, the distant sound of traffic. Sound you don't consciously notice — until it stops.

Importantly, embodiment in VR is a more fragile thing than presence. Bosman et al. (2025) found that while visual perspective strongly influences body ownership, audio perspective alone does not reliably change how grounded users feel in their virtual bodies.[3] This is actually useful design information: it tells me that the visual design of this space — how the room is framed, where the user stands, what they can and can't see — matters as much as the sound.

Gupta et al. (2025) add another layer: spatial familiarity in VR scenes improved vividness, accessibility, and emotional intensity of recalled memories.[4] The room in this piece is deliberately generic enough to feel like anyone's room — but specific enough to feel like a real one.

· · ·

How I plan to make it

The technical approach is straightforward — deliberately so. I want the process to stay in service of the feeling, not the other way around.

3D Scanning
Polycam / Reality Scan
Key objects — bed frame, desk, shelves
360° Capture
Insta360 X3
Single static shot from room centre
Sound Design
Phone + Rode Wireless GO
Room tone, distant traffic, fan hum
Scene Assembly
STYLY + Blender
Spatial audio, 360 + 3D layering

The STYLY scene layers optimised 3D scanned objects into a 360° photo backdrop, with spatial audio positioned around the space. The user is stationary — no locomotion, no interaction. The stillness is the point.

· · ·

What I'm worried about

The cliché problem. The biggest conceptual risk is sentimentality. A piece about a childhood bedroom leaving could easily become a nostalgia reel — warm filters, sweet music, nothing to push against. I want to hold something more ambiguous: the space should feel like it could be anyone's room, not a specific person's. The sound design needs to be quiet enough that the viewer supplies their own meaning.

Passive vs. incomplete. This is deliberately a non-interactive experience. The challenge is making that feel like a choice — not a limitation. There's a risk that viewers expect something to happen, and when nothing does, they read it as unfinished rather than intentional. Smith & Mulligan (2021) note that presence and emotional engagement in VR don't always map onto memory accuracy or felt completeness.[5] I take that as a reminder that the experience should be legible in the moment, not just in retrospect.

Technical precision. Aligning 3D scanned objects within a 360° photo backdrop is harder than it sounds — scale, lighting, and perspective all need to match convincingly. Polycam meshes come out very high-polygon; Blender cleanup is time-consuming. I need to be selective about what I scan, and what I leave implied.
