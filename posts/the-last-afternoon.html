Project 2 Proposal:
"The Last Afternoon"

A mixed reality experience reconstructing a moment suspended between existence and memory.

Photo via Unsplash — free to use

The Concept

There is a specific kind of grief that has no name — the feeling of standing in a room you are about to leave forever, knowing that every familiar object around you will soon belong to someone else. You can see everything clearly, but it already feels like a memory.

"The room won't exist anymore. But for 2–3 minutes in VR, you'll be the last person to stand in it."

"The Last Afternoon" is a mixed reality experience set inside a childhood bedroom on the final afternoon before a family moves out. The room is intact — furniture, scattered objects, afternoon light through the window — but it is also somehow already gone. The user stands in the centre of this space, unable to interact, simply present in a moment that is suspended between existence and memory.

The experience lasts approximately 2–3 minutes. There is no narrative arc, no beginning or end. Only the moment itself — ambient sound, soft light, the texture of familiar things — looping gently, like a photograph you can walk inside.

Connection to Course Concepts

Psychogeography & the Emotional Weight of Space

Guy Debord's psychogeography asks: how do specific spaces shape our emotions and behaviour? This project takes that question literally — it reconstructs a space that no longer exists, asking the viewer to feel its emotional weight through VR presence rather than physical navigation.

Where the dérive involves moving through unknown urban space, this project inverts that movement: the user is fixed in a deeply familiar space that has become strange. Immobility as a formal choice. The room you knew becomes the room you drift through emotionally.

Do-Ho Suh and the Portability of Home

The Korean artist Do-Ho Suh creates fabric replicas of architectural spaces — homes, corridors, staircases — that can be folded, packed, and carried. His work explores how a space continues to exist in the body and memory long after it is physically gone.

"The Last Afternoon" shares this impulse: using 3D scanning technology to carry a space into VR — not as documentation, but as an emotional artifact.

Henri Cartier-Bresson's Decisive Moment

Cartier-Bresson believed that photography could capture a decisive moment — a single instant where form, content, and meaning align perfectly. But what if a decisive moment is slow? What if it lasts an entire afternoon?

This project treats the 1–5 minute MR experience as an extended decisive moment — one that the viewer inhabits rather than observes.

Presence and Embodiment

The central design question is: what makes the user feel present in this space?

Rather than interaction or movement, I am interested in passive presence — the uncanny feeling of being placed into someone else's intimate memory. The spatial audio design will be critical here: the faint creak of the building, distant street sounds, the hum of an old fan. These ambient sound layers build embodiment — the sense that your body belongs in this space — without requiring any explicit interaction.

Technical Plan

Authoring Tools

Stage Tool
3D model cleanup & optimisation Blender (geometry cleanup, texture baking)
Scene assembly & spatial audio STYLY
360 video editing (if needed) Adobe Premiere Pro (equirectangular workflow)

STYLY Scene Design

  • 360° photo/video as the environmental backdrop
  • Optimised 3D scanned objects layered into the 360 space for depth and tactility
  • Spatial audio placed at key locations (window, door, fan) to create directional sound environment
  • Minimal or no locomotion — the user is stationary by design

Anticipated Challenges

Technical

  • 3D scan optimisation: Scanned meshes from Polycam are typically very high-poly. Blender retopology and texture baking are time-consuming — I need to be selective about which objects to scan.
  • 360 + 3D object alignment: Placing 3D objects accurately within a 360 photo background requires careful matching of scale, lighting, and perspective.
  • Spatial audio in STYLY: Testing audio falloff requires iteration in-headset, depending on equipment loan availability.

Conceptual

  • Avoiding nostalgia cliché: The risk is that the piece becomes sentimental rather than experiential. The design needs to hold ambiguity — this could be anyone's room, anyone's last afternoon.
  • Passive vs. exploratory: This is deliberately a non-interactive experience. The challenge is making stillness feel intentional rather than incomplete.

Development from Project 1

Project 1 focused on capturing the surface qualities of a space — its visual and physical presence. Project 2 moves into the interior of a space: its emotional, temporal, and memorial dimensions. Where Project 1 asked "what does this place look like?", Project 2 asks "what does it feel like to have been here?"

Technically, this builds on 3D scanning and STYLY skills developed in Project 1, but extends into spatial audio design and 360 integration — areas I plan to develop specifically for this project.
